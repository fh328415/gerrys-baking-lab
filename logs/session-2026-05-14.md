## Ingredients

| Ingredient                        | Quantity |
|-----------------------------------|----------|
| **Bread flour** (12.7% protein)   | 2¼ cups  |
| Brown sugar                       | 1 cup    |
| White sugar                       | ½ cup    |
| Unsalted butter, room temperature | 1 cup    |
| Large eggs                        | 2        |
| Pure vanilla extract              | 1 tsp    |
| Baking soda                       | 1 tsp    |
| Fine sea salt                     | 1 tsp    |
| Semi-sweet chocolate chips        | 2 cups   |

> **Control batch:** identical recipe with all-purpose flour (~10.5% protein). Brown sugar retained from session 2026-04-10 findings.

## Procedure

1. Preheated oven to 375°F / 190°C. Lined two baking sheets with parchment paper.
2. Sifted bread flour with baking soda and salt. Set aside.
3. Creamed butter with sugars on medium speed until light and fluffy (~3 min).
4. Added eggs one at a time, then vanilla extract.
5. Gradually incorporated dry mixture. Mixed 20 seconds longer than usual to ensure gluten development (deliberately — this is the mechanism being tested).
6. Folded in chocolate chips.
7. Portioned into 2-tablespoon rounds; spaced 5 cm apart.
8. Baked for 11 minutes. Cookies held their shape notably better during baking.
9. Cooled on wire rack 10 minutes before evaluation. Prepared and baked control in parallel.

## Observed Results

- **Experimental (bread flour):** Final spread ~3.7 cm — the least spread of any session so far. Slightly lighter color than the brown-sugar-only baseline. Texture: noticeably chewier, with a more elastic bite. Edges were crisp and defined. Interior remained moist.
- **Control (all-purpose flour):** Final spread ~4.2 cm. Softer, more uniform texture. Less resistance when bitten.
- The bread flour dough felt noticeably stiffer when portioning — consistent with increased gluten development.
- No significant flavor difference was detected between the two batches at this stage.

## Conclusions

Hypothesis **confirmed**. Bread flour produced the chewiest texture of any single-variable test conducted so far:

1. **Gluten network strength:** Bread flour (12.7% protein) forms more and stronger gluten strands compared to all-purpose flour (10.5%), creating a more elastic dough that resists spread and holds moisture during baking.
2. **Controlled spread:** The stronger network also means cookies held their shape better in the oven — an unexpected but welcome aesthetic improvement.
3. **Neutral flavor impact:** Switching flour type did not noticeably alter flavor, confirming that the chewiness effect is structural rather than chemical.

**Recommended next step (Session 2026-05-28):** Combine the three highest-impact interventions identified so far — brown butter (Session 4), bread flour (this session), and 48-hour cold rest (Session 2) — into a single optimized batch. This is a standard experimental approach: first test variables independently, then test in combination to check for synergistic or antagonistic interactions.
